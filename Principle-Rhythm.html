Rhythm

Rhythm creates movement through repetition and variation of elements. It helps guide the viewer's eye through a design and can set the overall tempo or mood.


Types of Rhythm

  • Regular rhythm: Repeating elements with equal spacing — stable and predictable.
  • Flowing rhythm: Organic or curved repetition that suggests natural movement.
  • Progressive rhythm: Systematic change in an element (size, color, spacing) that implies motion or direction.
  • Alternating rhythm: Two or more elements alternate in a pattern (A-B-A-B) to create interest.

How to create rhythm

Use repetition, spacing, scale, color, texture, and direction. Small variations between repeats keep the rhythm engaging — exact repetition can feel static.


Visual cues & techniques

  • Repetition: Repeating motifs, shapes, or colors.
  • Spacing (tempo): Consistent spacing feels like a steady beat; varied spacing creates syncopation.
  • Scale: Gradual size changes create progression.
  • Direction: Lines and shapes that lead the eye form a visual path.
  • Contrast: Alternating light/dark or textured/smooth areas emphasize beats.

Exercises

  1. Analyze: Find three images (web, print, photo). Identify the type of rhythm present and list which elements create it.
  2. Create Alternating: Make a simple grid where two shapes alternate (A-B-A-B). Adjust spacing and note how the rhythm changes.
  3. Create Progressive: Design a row of five shapes that change in size and/or color to show progression.


Quick activity (10 minutes)

Create three 600×200 strips (or draw on paper): one with regular rhythm, one with alternating rhythm, and one with progressive rhythm. Compare which one best guides your eye and why.
