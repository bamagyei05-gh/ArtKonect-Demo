Texture

What is Texture?

Texture describes the surface quality of an object — how it might feel to the touch (actual/tactile texture) or how it appears to feel visually (implied/visual texture). Texture adds depth, interest, and realism to artworks and designs.

  • Actual (Tactile) Texture: Real surface properties you can feel (e.g., rough, smooth, bumpy).
  • Implied (Visual) Texture: An illusion of texture created through shading, pattern, or brushwork.
  • Texture in Layouts: Using patterns, backgrounds, or material cues to create contrast and hierarchy.
Example: A photograph of wood shows tactile grain; a digital pattern can imply the same texture visually.

Why Texture Matters

Texture creates tactile interest and can influence mood. It can also help with readability when used subtly in UI backgrounds or to separate sections in a composition.

Exercises

  1. Create a study of three textures (smooth, rough, patterned) using pencil or digital brushes.
  2. Design a layout that uses a subtle textured background to separate header and content areas without reducing readability.
  3. Create an implied texture using hatching or stippling techniques in a small compositional sketch.
